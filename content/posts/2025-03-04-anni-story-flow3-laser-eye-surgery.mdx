## Minus 1.75 sounds small. Until it isn't.

Short-sightedness has a way of creeping into everything. A small prescription might not sound like much on paper. But when it stops you seeing road signs clearly, or makes the cinema blurry, or means you can't recognize a friend across the street—it becomes a daily inconvenience.

This is Anni's story. She's a surgical nurse at KSA Silmakeskus, mother of two, and the person who finally decided to stop living behind glasses.

### From Banking to Healthcare—and Finding KSA

In February 2024, Anni started nursing studies at Tallinn Health Care College. The opportunity to work at KSA came through the school, and she applied without hesitation.

Before that, she'd worked across very different fields—banking, construction, office roles. Long days at screens. Routine. At some point, she felt pulled toward something more meaningful. Her family had someone who needed ongoing medical care, and that experience planted a seed. She wanted to work in healthcare.

**Long days behind screens in banking had tired her eyes. But only when she started at KSA did Anni realize something else: her myopia itself needed addressing.**

She'd worn minus glasses since 10th grade. Never tried contacts. The prescription was small, but just large enough to:

- Miss traffic signs from a distance
- Lose detail in films
- Struggle to recognize people on the street

She still laughs about a restaurant visit when she'd left her glasses at home. The waiter's confusion was real. Embarrassing then. Funny now.

_Sound familiar? Rain, sleet, fogged lenses, limited view. This was Anni's normal—until she decided it didn't have to be._

### Trust Grows From What You See

Working daily with patients, Anni watched the results of [Flow3 laser surgery](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) firsthand. She heard colleagues share their own experiences. Trust built gradually, naturally.

"The only thing I regret," she says now, "is not deciding sooner."

On the day of her procedure, she felt certain. She knew exactly what would happen. She trusted the team completely.

The real push came from a conversation with a colleague optometrist, who showed her what her vision could be without glasses. Not imagined. Concrete. Clear.

### The Day of the Procedure—Patient and Professional at Once

Procedure day was calm. Anni knew what to expect. Because she'd booked her surgery for the evening, she worked normally that morning—which somehow made the whole thing feel less like a big event and more like simply choosing better vision.

The most memorable moment came a few days later. She was driving when she suddenly realized: she could see the road signs clearly from a distance. Without glasses. That's when it became real.

"Why didn't I do this before?" she remembers thinking. "How much money did I waste on glasses all those years?"

It wasn't just about vision correction. It was about reclaiming small moments—noticing things she'd stopped expecting to see. Freedom she hadn't even realized she was missing.

### What Changed

No more searching for glasses in the morning. No more fogged lenses in rain. No more squinting at screens or cinema screens or street signs. No more the tiny frustration of a daily dependency.

For someone working in an eye clinic, there's an extra layer to the decision. Anni sees the technology, meets the surgeon, knows the science. She chose Flow3 because she works here—and because the entire clinical team at KSA chose it for their own eyes. That's not marketing. That's trust in action.
