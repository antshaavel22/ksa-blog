## Who is Agnes?

Agnes is the sales director at KSA Silmakeskus. Every day, she manages people and processes. What she values most is her team and the chance to help people grow. Beyond work, Agnes lives for sport and movement—high-intensity training is her passion, and she's always made time to stay active.

But for 15 years, she did all this while depending on glasses and contact lenses. That changed.

## Glasses since age 12

Agnes got her first pair of glasses at twelve. Contact lenses followed. Fifteen years of dependency.

Over time, wearing lenses stopped feeling like a choice—it became a burden. "The worst moments were when I had only one lens," Agnes recalls. "Plus there was constant mental load: Did I pack a spare pair? Do I have solution? Is the case in my bag?" Every trip required planning around her vision.

Her clearest memory of lens frustration came during a trip to Mexico. Her existing lenses degraded partway through. She had no backup. Local pharmacies only stocked weaker prescriptions. "I spent half the holiday half-blind," she says.

## Why now?

Working at an eye clinic meant Agnes already knew the options. But the real trigger was simple: she wanted her life back.

"I decided to get rid of glasses so I could enjoy life without constantly planning. No more wondering if I have everything I need to see the world clearly."

Sport and travel without logistics. That was the dream.

## Recovery: faster than expected

Agnes's recovery was remarkably smooth. "One week after the [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/), I had 100% vision clarity. No pain, no discomfort—nothing like that. The only thing I felt for a couple of days was slight blurriness up close."

She returned to her routine quickly. "Even though I took the first week off from work and heavy training, I wasn't inactive. I walked, cooked, spent time with loved ones. Life just continued."

That's the beauty of [Flow3](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/)—flapless, surface-level laser correction. No flap means faster healing. No flap means safer for athletes.

## Life after Flow3

The freedom shows up in small moments now.

"It's genuinely pleasant to go to bed without thinking about whether I removed my lenses. It leaves room for spontaneous naps—something that barely happened before."

Agnes emphasizes what matters most: independence and autonomy. No longer managing glasses or lenses, she's regained control of her day.

To anyone hesitating—anyone scared of making the decision—Agnes has one piece of advice: **"Do it scared."**

Freedom usually requires a small leap.
