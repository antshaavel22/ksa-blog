## 8–12 minutes every day. Sounds small? Think again.

You wake up. Search for your glasses on the nightstand—or was it the desk? Put them on your nose. Clean them because they're fogged. At night, back in the case. If you wear contacts: wash your hands, open the case, solution, insertion, removal at night, case, fresh solution. Every day. Every morning and every evening. For years and decades on end.

On its own, it's not terrible—8–12 minutes a day. But multiply that by 365 days and then by 30 years, and you get a number worth serious thought: **over 100 days of your life** spent fussing with glasses and lenses. That's more than three months—around the clock, 24 hours a day.

## The lost glasses marathon

According to the American Optometric Association (AOA), **27% of glasses wearers lose their glasses at least once a week**. Average search time? 5 minutes and 20 seconds. Seems trivial, but let's do the math:

* Once a week × 5 min 20 sec = roughly **4.6 hours per year**
* But many lose them more often—realistically 2–3 times weekly
* Up to **17 hours per year** just searching for glasses—that's more than two working days

And that's only the searching. Add in the daily cleaning, wiping fogged lenses in winter, the morning and evening contact lens ritual—and the time adds up dramatically.

## Daily frustrations you've simply learned to tolerate

Glasses and contacts aren't just a time drain. They're a thousand small inconveniences you've grown so used to over the years that you hardly notice them anymore:

* **Fogged lenses when coming in from the cold**—entering shops, opening the oven in the kitchen, the sauna. In Estonia, where winter lasts half the year, this is daily reality.
* **Raindrops on your lenses**—seeing the world through a veil of water droplets. The lens-wiping motion is automatic by now.
* **Can't spontaneously jump in the pool**—an unplanned swim? Forget it without your contacts. Kids are calling from the water—you're fishing for your aids.
* **Contact solution at the airport**—the 100 ml rule, separate bags, remembering to pack them. And if you forget—you're at your destination without lenses.
* **Dry eyes by evening**—after 10 hours of lens wear, your eyes turn red and dry. You use more eye drops than you'd like.
* **Falling asleep with lenses in**—and waking up with sandpaper eyes. A familiar feeling?
* **Exercising in glasses**—they slide, fog up, get in the way. Every squat means pushing them back up.

You've adapted to all this. But should you have to?
