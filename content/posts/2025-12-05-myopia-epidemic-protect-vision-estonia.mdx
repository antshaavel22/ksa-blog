## The Myopia Epidemic: Why Half the World Will Be Shortsighted

Shortsightedness—or myopia—is no longer just a schoolchild's problem. It's one of the fastest-spreading health conditions on the planet. Screens are reshaping our eyes. Vision in adults is getting worse without warning. And the question isn't *if* you should consider a lasting solution—it's *when*.

According to the World Health Organization, by 2050, half the global population will be myopic. That's roughly 5 billion people. The rise is most dramatic in children: between 1990 and 2023, myopia rates nearly tripled. But this isn't just a young person's crisis. In Europe, about one in three young adults already wears correction for distance vision.

The numbers are climbing. And they won't stop unless we understand what's actually happening inside the eye.

## What's Actually Happening to Your Eyes?

Myopia isn't cosmetic. It's structural.

The eye becomes too long—the eyeball itself elongates. Light from distant objects focuses in front of the retina instead of directly on it. Result: distant objects blur; close objects stay sharp.

"Most people think shortsightedness is just inconvenient—you need glasses or contacts," say the optometrists at KSA Silmakeskus. "But it's a physical change in the eye's anatomy. That matters. Because the shape of your eye affects your long-term eye health."

This isn't about prescription strength. It's about the fundamental geometry of your eye changing, permanently.

## Screen Time and Indoor Living: The Real Culprits

Why do eyes elongate in the first place?

Screen time tops the list. A large study tracking over 335,000 people found that each additional hour of screen time per day increases myopia risk by roughly 21%. At four hours daily—nearly standard for office work now—the risk almost doubles.

Natural daylight is protective. During the pandemic, when children spent less time outdoors and screen time jumped from 2.1 to 5.6 hours daily, myopia progression accelerated sharply. Even 1–2 hours outside each day makes a measurable difference.

The message is simple: eyes need sunlight. Offices and homes don't provide it. That's why the myopia curve keeps climbing.

## The Myth: Vision Stabilises After Age 20

Common belief: your eyes stop changing after 20.

It's wrong.

Over 90% of adult-onset myopia develops between ages 18 and 30. Research shows that 14% of young adults with normal vision at 20 become shortsighted by 28.

"Regular eye checks matter—even when your vision feels fine," KSA's optometrists emphasise. "Myopia can progress slowly. You might not notice until the change is significant."

Skip the checkups, and you might miss the window to act.

## Why High Myopia Is Different

It's tempting to dismiss myopia as merely inconvenient. Wear glasses. Problem solved.

But high myopia—typically -4.00 diopters and beyond—carries real health risks. The elongated eye has greater risk of retinal detachment, myopic macular degeneration, and early cataracts. These aren't minor annoyances. They can threaten sight.

That's when a permanent vision correction becomes not just convenient—it becomes medically sensible.
