## A Mother's Struggle

Liis Toomjõe knows what many parents worry about: the weight of poor vision in everyday life. As a young mother and habitual glasses wearer, she faced a constant, unsettling anxiety. At the swimming pool. In the sauna. Anywhere her sight blurred without correction.

"Without my glasses, everything was foggy," she says frankly. "I'd be watching my child and terrified they'd disappear from view. Now I swim, I bathe, I see my daughter clearly. Every time."

Working as a customer service representative and seamstress, Liis had learned glasses weren't just an inconvenience—they were a genuine problem. Driving in poor light left her exhausted. They'd slip down her nose constantly. In winter, stepping from cold air into a warm room meant fogged lenses, creating awkward, embarrassing moments. She'd tried contact lenses, but her prescription was uneven (−1.5 in one eye, −1.75 in the other), and with a corneal curvature of 9.0, she could only wear extended-wear lenses—limited to eight hours daily. Eventually they deteriorated so badly she couldn't wear them comfortably.

"There was always this sensation of something being in my eye," she recalls. "And I couldn't even wear sunglasses properly. Everything stayed blurry."

## The Moment Everything Changed

Liis remembers one particular evening vividly. She'd attended a social gathering with friends, come home exhausted, and fallen asleep immediately. The next morning, she woke and opened her eyes.

Suddenly, she could see.

Crystal clear. Without correction.

She'd forgotten to remove her contact lenses. But that moment—that *sensation*—of seeing the world sharply, naturally, without aid: goosebumps rose across her skin. "That 'wow' feeling," she says, "made me realise what I was missing."

Like many vision patients, Liis waited longer than she needed to. Years passed. Broken frames. Lost glasses. Days when she simply couldn't drive safely. Until one afternoon, something broke inside her—not her glasses, but her patience.

"My frames snapped. Then they bent. Then I lost them entirely and couldn't even drive to replace them. I thought: enough. I want to see the world with my own eyes. No more."
