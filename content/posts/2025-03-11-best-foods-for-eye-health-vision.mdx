Did you know that dry eyes can often improve simply by changing what you eat? Why is the blueberry considered nature's eye supplement? And can spinach and kale really help prevent serious eye disease?

We've put together a practical guide to the foods that genuinely support your vision — with the science to back it up.

## The Connection Between Diet and Eye Health

We all know that movement, stress management, sleep and good nutrition matter for overall wellbeing. But eyes? Eyes are often forgotten.

Yet your eyes are complex organs. To stay healthy and function well throughout your life, they need a diverse range of nutrients. The question isn't whether diet affects your vision — it absolutely does. The question is: which foods deliver the biggest benefit?

## Omega-3 Fatty Acids for Dry Eyes

Dry eyes are common. Your eyes either aren't producing enough tears, or the tears lack the right composition to protect the surface. The result: scratchiness, irritation, excessive tearing, blurred vision.

Omega-3 fatty acids — found in salmon, mackerel, flaxseeds, chia seeds, avocados, tofu, and walnuts — can help. These healthy fats support tear production and reduce inflammation in the eye. For anyone struggling with dry eye syndrome, adding more omega-3 sources to your diet is a straightforward first step.

## Leafy Greens for Macular Degeneration Prevention

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is one of the world's most common eye conditions and the leading cause of vision loss in people over 50.

Dark leafy greens — spinach, kale, collard greens, broccoli — are rich in two crucial carotenoids: lutein and zeaxanthin. These compounds accumulate in the retina and act as a protective shield against AMD. The science here is solid: eating these vegetables regularly is one of the most effective dietary ways to reduce your risk.

## Orange Foods for Night Vision

Beta-carotene is converted to vitamin A in your body. Carrots, sweet potatoes, and winter squash are packed with it.

Why does this matter? Vitamin A is essential for producing rhodopsin, a pigment in your eyes that allows you to see in low light. Better night vision starts with what's on your plate.

## Citrus Fruits to Help Prevent Cataracts

Vitamin C is a powerful antioxidant. Oranges, grapefruits, and lemons contain it in abundance.

Cataracts — clouding of the eye's lens — develop gradually with age. Vitamin C may help reduce that risk by protecting your lens from oxidative damage. Think of it as preventive care, one orange at a time.

## Nuts and Seeds for Antioxidant Protection

Almonds and sunflower seeds are excellent sources of vitamin E. Working alongside vitamin C and other antioxidants, vitamin E protects your eyes from oxidative stress and cellular damage.

A simple daily handful of nuts or seeds is an easy way to add this protection.

## Blueberries for Sharp Vision

Right now, forests are full of blueberries. We recommend eating them daily — because the evidence for their eye health benefits is remarkable.

Blueberries are loaded with anthocyanins, powerful antioxidants that may improve visual acuity, reduce eye strain, and support overall retinal health. They're not a magic cure, but they're about as close as food gets.

## The Bigger Picture

Nutrition matters. It won't replace professional eye care — regular check-ups are still essential. But what you eat directly influences how your eyes age and function.

If you're considering vision correction through [laser eye surgery](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) or [lens replacement](https://ksa.ee/en/icb-time), maintaining good eye health before and after your procedure makes sense. And that starts with the basics: omega-3s, leafy greens, antioxidants, and yes — blueberries.

Your eyes will thank you.
