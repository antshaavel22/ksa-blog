## Fifteen Years Behind Glass

Polina got her first pair of glasses at 15. She wore contact lenses occasionally—mainly for sports or special events—but glasses were her daily reality.

The frustration? The same frames, day after day.

"When you wear the same glasses every single day, the frames get boring fast. You constantly want to buy new ones—and that adds up quickly," she says.

Contacts weren't the answer either. "They dried out my eyes, and every morning took forever to put them in properly."

Then came the trip that stuck with her. "I went on holiday with a friend and her child. I carelessly left my glasses on a shelf, and the kid broke them in half. I had no spares, and I wasn't using contacts yet. For the remaining five days of my holiday, I couldn't see people's faces properly. I experienced the whole trip through photos afterward."

It's the kind of moment that makes you wonder: *there has to be another way*.

Working at KSA meant Polina saw firsthand what her colleagues had chosen. Most of the clinical team had already undergone laser vision correction—for their own eyes.

"When the opportunity came up, I didn't hesitate. I booked the procedure straight away," she says.

## Recovery: Smooth and Fast

The first week unfolded calmly. On days one through four, Polina experienced something unusual: the opposite of her old problem. Distance vision was sharp; close-up vision was blurry.

"After four days, both balanced out. Suddenly I could see equally well at distance and up close," she recalls.

Those early days had a gentle rhythm. "I walked a lot and handled housework. The main difference was barely touching my phone or computer. That digital detox actually did me a lot of good," she says.

Recovery from [Flow3](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) takes roughly a week—no flap, no complications, back to normal life quickly.

## The New Freedom

Even a few days after surgery, the habit lingered: reaching to adjust glasses that weren't there anymore, preparing to remove them before bed.

But those small adjustments fade fast.

What matters now? "Swimming and the water park. I can dive, go to the sauna—no glasses, no worries. That freedom is worth everything."

For someone who spent 15 years managing frames, spares, and the constant question of what to do with glasses during any activity in water, this isn't a small thing.

It's the difference between watching your life through a restriction and actually living it.
