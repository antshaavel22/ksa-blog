## The Fear That Stops So Many

We meet them regularly at KSA Silmakeskus—people who've worn glasses or contact lenses for years, desperate to see clearly without them, yet gripped by apprehension. The fear of laser eye surgery is real. Often, it stems not from medicine but from the unknown, or half-remembered stories that took on a life of their own.

Keit Ausner was one of them. A few years ago, she would have sworn she'd never have the procedure done. Today? She calls it the best decision of her life.

## Meet Keit

Keit is the kind of person who lights up a room—energetic, sharp, relentlessly positive. She's spent her career in marketing and communications, rising through Äripäev (Estonia's business newspaper) from marketing management to head of digital strategy. Outside of work, she hosts a podcast for Äripäev radio featuring inspiring stories and marketing innovation. In her spare time, she reads voraciously, renovates her home, and makes things with her hands. Oh, and she has two cats named See (This) and Teine (That).

She came to KSA after years of glasses and contact lenses. The desire for clear vision had finally outweighed her hesitation. But underneath the hope was something stronger: fear.

## "I Like to Be in Control"

Keit is honest about her relationship with medical procedures. "I'm naturally quite anxious," she admits with a laugh. "Though I prefer to call it 'liking to be in control.' Once, as a teenager, I literally stood up and walked out of my doctor's office when he suggested taking a blood sample on the spot. I rebooked for the next day because I simply wasn't ready. That's just how I am."

That need for control, that wariness around the unexpected—it's a big part of what made the thought of eye surgery feel so daunting.

## A Lifetime of Hating Glasses

"I've hated my glasses my entire life," Keit says without exaggeration. "I got them young, and from day one they felt like an inconvenience. I've always been quite active—even a bit clumsy—and glasses just got in the way." The small frustrations added up: constantly pushing them back up her nose, the fog on her lenses stepping onto a cold bus in winter, the awkward moment of standing there pretending nothing's wrong while waiting for the fog to clear.

In her twenties, contact lenses felt like a solution. "For a while, life felt normal again. I even started wearing eye makeup," she recalls. That relief lasted nearly a decade.

Then things changed. Her eyes tired easily. The lenses became uncomfortable within hours. Within a couple of years, her eyes were dry and irritated by midday. And there was another problem she didn't want to discuss, but knew couldn't be ignored.

## Breaking Through the Barrier

For years, the idea of [laser eye surgery](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) sat in the back of Keit's mind—something she wanted but couldn't imagine doing. The fear was real, but so was the growing frustration with her eyes.

What changed? At some point, the discomfort of staying the same became greater than the fear of change.

When Keit finally came in for a consultation, she encountered a team that understood her anxiety wasn't weakness—it was information. Dr. Ants Haavel and the clinical team took time to explain exactly what would happen, why the [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) was gentler than older methods, and what to expect in each moment.

One detail struck her: the entire clinical team at KSA had chosen Flow3 for their own vision. These weren't people who did the procedure because it was profitable. They trusted it enough to use it themselves. That mattered.

## The Day It Happened

Keit walked into the procedure room with a racing heart. She's not the type to pretend otherwise. But she was also prepared—she knew what was coming, and that made all the difference.

The [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) itself took minutes. No blade. No flap. Just gentle laser reshaping of the cornea's surface. She felt no pain—only a little pressure, some strange sensations she'd been warned about. Her clinical team talked her through every second.

When it was over, Keit opened her eyes to a clarity she hadn't experienced since childhood.

## "The Best Decision of My Life"

That's what she calls it now. And she means it.

The recovery was straightforward—a week of being a bit more careful than usual, then a life transformed. No more glasses. No more lenses. No more frustration. Just the freedom to be as active and clumsy as she is without thinking twice about it.

Does Keit still consider herself cautious? Absolutely. Does she still like to feel in control? Completely. But now she knows that sometimes the bravest thing you can do is move toward what frightens you—especially when the people guiding you clearly know what they're doing.

## The Truth About Fear

If you're afraid of laser eye surgery, Keit wouldn't tell you to ignore that feeling. She'd just tell you this: fear is often loudest when we don't have complete information. The moment you really understand what's happening—the moment you sit down with someone who's done this thousands of times and hear what actually occurs—the fear usually shrinks to something manageable.

And what you find on the other side? For Keit, it was freedom. Clear vision. The chance to move through the world without an accessory she'd never wanted in the first place.
