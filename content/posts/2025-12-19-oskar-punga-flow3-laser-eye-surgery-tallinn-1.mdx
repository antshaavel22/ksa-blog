## Meet Oskar

Oskar is an actor at Viljandi Ugala Theatre. His life moves between theatre rehearsals, snowboarding runs, hiking trails, cycling routes, ping pong matches with friends, and baking bread in his kitchen. He solves crosswords, walks his dog, and genuinely enjoys discovery. Life is full. And now that his vision is sharp and effortless, he experiences it all with even more clarity.

## Fifteen years of small compromises

Oskar got his first pair of glasses in middle school. Sitting at the back of the classroom, the blackboard was just a blur. As a sporty teenager who played football, switching to contact lenses felt like the obvious next step.

Then came 15 years of routine. Contact lens solution. Backup pairs left at home. A lens popping out mid-day with no spare in sight. Small irritations that pile up. Over time, Oskar stopped seeing them as temporary fixes and started seeing them as his normal life. But that normal, he decided, didn't have to be permanent.

## The turning point

When Oskar turned 30, the question finally crystallized: *Why?*

Why keep managing lenses and glasses when clear vision without them was actually possible?

"I started doing the math," he says with a slight smile. "If I'm lucky enough to live a long life, I could spend it all seeing perfectly—without having to remind myself every morning that I need my lenses. No more backup glasses, no more containers marked with blue L and white R. All of that could just... stop."

He'd also heard from friends who'd had the [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) at KSA Silmakeskus. Positive experiences. Straightforward. That made the decision simple.

## The procedure and recovery

On the day of surgery, Oskar felt a brief moment of discomfort. Nothing more.

By the next morning, everything felt better than expected. His friends had warned him about a tough recovery—weeks of caution and inconvenience. His reality was different. Everything just kept improving.

He was back at theatre rehearsals the next day. Three weeks later, he was performing on stage under bright spotlights, seeing the audience in sharp detail.

## Life after

The change is subtle but constant.

Now when Oskar looks out his window at Lake Viljandi, the view feels more vivid—more real somehow. Theatre performances have a clarity he'd forgotten about. And one of his favourite small moments: spotting a friend across the room and being the first to wave hello. No more guessing. No more blur excusing a missed greeting.

For someone who spent 15 years managing his vision, the freedom to simply *see* turns out to be its own kind of joy.
