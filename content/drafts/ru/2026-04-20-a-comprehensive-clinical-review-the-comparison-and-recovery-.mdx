Refractive surgery has gone through three generations: surface ablation (TransPRK, sold commercially as Flow3), flap-based ablation (LASIK), and small-incision lenticule extraction (SMILE). In the marketing, the conversation usually stops at recovery speed. In a clinic, the more interesting questions are about long-term corneal stability and visual quality. After more than 55,000 touch-free, cut-free laser procedures at KSA, here is how the three methods actually compare.

## What happens in the operating room

Across all three methods, time in the laser room is short, around 5 to 7 minutes per eye. With Flow3, the excimer laser itself runs for only 10 to 30 seconds, working on the surface of the cornea (the eye's clear front layer). LASIK and SMILE both involve a physical incision and deeper work in the corneal stroma; Flow3 does not touch or cut anything inside the cornea.

There is one quirk only Flow3 has: patients see a real, objective improvement before they leave the room. Most walk out 15 minutes later seeing better than they came in.

## The honest first week

Flow3's recovery is not painless. Day one is usually clear and comfortable. Days two and three are the hard part: the eyes water and itch, and most patients describe a feeling like chopping onions. This is the surface layer (the epithelium) growing back from the edge of the cornea inward.

By day five or six the protective bandage lenses come off, the discomfort stops within seconds, and vision is usually 0.8 or better. By day seven all physical restrictions are lifted, including contact sports and swimming. With LASIK and SMILE those restrictions stay in place for several weeks because of the flap or incision.

## Long-term visual quality

Most of the visual recovery happens between weeks two and five. Around 95% of patients reach 20/20. The more interesting changes happen between months three and six, as the cornea remodels and the corneal nerves reconnect.

A 2025 BMC Ophthalmology study put numbers on this. At six months, total higher-order aberrations were lowest in the TransPRK group at 0.25 ± 0.07 µm, compared with 0.30 ± 0.08 µm for SMILE and 0.38 ± 0.09 µm for FS-LASIK. Spherical aberration was lowest with TransPRK (+0.05 µm) versus SMILE (+0.08 µm) and LASIK (+0.15 µm). Coma was also lowest with TransPRK at 0.12 ± 0.04 µm versus 0.22 ± 0.06 µm for FS-LASIK.

## Night vision: where the optical zone matters

Those numbers translate directly into night vision and contrast sensitivity. In dim light at 3 cd/m², TransPRK had higher contrast sensitivity than SMILE across all spatial frequencies (3, 6, 12, 18 cpd). The reason is geometric: Flow3 uses larger optical treatment zones, so when your pupil dilates in the dark, the treated area still covers it.

With SMILE, removing the lenticule can leave small irregularities in the stroma, which show up as slightly more aberration in dim light.

## Corneal biomechanics and the zero-ectasia question

A question that comes up often: how has Flow3 maintained a zero-ectasia rate across 21 years and over 55,000 procedures? The answer is structural. Reported ectasia incidence after LASIK is around 90 per 100,000 procedures and after SMILE roughly 11 per 100,000.

Published in silico and ex vivo work shows that creating a LASIK flap permanently weakens the cornea: the flap interface only regains 2.4% to 28.1% of original tensile strength. Surface ablation does not create a flap and does not enter the deeper stroma, so it preserves the cornea's structural continuity and removes most of the biomechanical risk for ectasia.

## The bottom line

LASIK gives the best day-two visual experience, but at the cost of a lifelong flap and a higher long-term complication risk. Flow3 asks for patience during the first week, and in return gives you a cornea that stays structurally intact and optically clean for the rest of your life. Which trade-off is right depends on the patient. The job of an [eye doctor](https://ksa.ee) is to lay both options out honestly and help you choose.
