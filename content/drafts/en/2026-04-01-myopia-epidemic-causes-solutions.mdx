## The Numbers Tell a Stark Story

Myopia—or short-sightedness—is no longer just a minor inconvenience. It's become one of the fastest-growing health crises on the planet. The World Health Organization projects that by 2050, roughly half of humanity—around 5 billion people—will be myopic. The acceleration is particularly sharp among children and young adults.

A landmark meta-analysis published in the British Journal of Ophthalmology examined 276 studies covering 5.4 million participants. The findings: childhood myopia rates have **tripled between 1990 and 2023**. In South Korea, 75.8% of people aged 18–39 are myopic. Recent data from The Lancet Regional Health (2025) shows that nearly one in three young European adults—32.74%—sees blurrily without glasses or contact lenses. Even more sobering: a BMJ Public Health analysis warns that by 2035, myopia-related complications will become the leading cause of irreversible vision loss in Western Europe.

## Why Is Vision Getting Worse?

The mechanism is straightforward: the eyeball grows too long, and light focuses in front of the retina instead of directly on it. It's a physical change, not just a number on a prescription.

Screen time is the primary driver. A meta-analysis from JAMA Network Open reviewed 45 studies across 335,000 participants and found that **each additional hour in front of a screen daily increases myopia risk by 21%**. At four hours a day, the risk nearly doubles. When COVID-19 lockdowns hit, children's daily screen time jumped from 2.1 to 5.6 hours. The result: accelerated myopia development. VSP Vision Care research shows that 43% of remote workers noticed vision decline after the pandemic—some spending up to 13 hours daily in front of screens.

There's a persistent myth that vision stabilises by age 20. It doesn't. A JAMA Ophthalmology study following people from age 20 to 28 found that 14% of those with normal vision at 20 developed myopia by 28. Equally striking: 37.8% experienced clinically significant vision decline. According to the International Myopia Institute, over 90% of myopia that develops in adulthood appears between ages 18 and 30—not earlier.

## High Myopia: When Glasses Aren't Enough

High myopia—anything from -5 to -6 diopters and beyond—carries serious health risks that spectacles cannot address. A 2023 Nature study showed that high myopia increases the risk of retinal detachment **39 times higher** than normal vision. Each additional diopter raises glaucoma risk by 20%. At -6 diopters, the risk of macular degeneration jumps 41-fold, and cataract surgery becomes 3.4 times more likely. BMJ Public Health forecasts that by 2075, myopia complications will affect 3.6 million Europeans. As one optometrist at KSA puts it: "Regular eye checks matter even when your eyes feel fine—vision loss can be gradual and invisible until it's too late."
