## Screen time is straining our eyes—but not always for the reason you think

Digital eye strain touches 65% of regular screen users. Blurred vision, dryness, headaches—the symptoms are real. But here's the uncomfortable truth: those trendy blue light filter glasses? The science doesn't back them up.

The numbers are clear. As screen time climbs, so do complaints. An [NIH review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9434525/) found that 50–65% of adults experience digital eye strain. Post-COVID, some studies recorded rates as high as 94%. But what's actually going on, and what can you do about it?

## The real culprit: we simply blink less

Here's something most people miss: when you're concentrating on a screen, your blink rate plummets. Normally, we blink 14–22 times a minute. In front of a screen? That drops to just 3–7 times. Your eyes dry out, the tear film breaks down, and you get that burning, gritty sensation.

But screen brightness isn't the only factor. Posture matters. Screen distance matters. Lighting matters. Walk into any office and you'll see people hunched forward, screens too close, necks craned. By evening, their eyes feel completely exhausted.

Our optometrists see this pattern constantly. "Patients tell us their eyes are shot by 5pm," one of our team notes. "When we dig deeper, it's almost always the same story: they're not blinking enough, and the screen is too close. Simple adjustments make a real difference."

## The 20-20-20 rule works

There's a reason this gets recommended worldwide: **every 20 minutes, take a 20-second break and look at something 20 feet away** (roughly 6 metres). It lets your eyes relax and naturally triggers more blinking.

The research supports it—not as a cure, but as a practical habit that genuinely reduces strain. Combined with conscious blinking, proper screen distance (at least 50–60 cm), and decent lighting, most people notice relief surprisingly quickly.

## What blue light glasses actually don't do

The market is flooded with products promising to "protect your eyes from blue light." But according to that same [NIH review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9434525/), the evidence simply isn't there. Blue light blocking glasses haven't been shown to reduce digital eye strain symptoms. The headaches, dryness, and blurred vision you're experiencing? Those are usually physiological—caused by how you're using the screen, not the light itself.

Blue light glasses aren't harmful. But don't rely on them as your solution. Regular breaks, screen brightness adjustments, and proper dry eye care usually deliver far more benefit.

## When to take it seriously

If eye strain persists despite good screen habits, or if you're noticing persistent dryness, it's worth getting checked. Sometimes what feels like "screen fatigue" is actually an underlying vision issue—refractive error, focusing problems, or dry eye disease. A proper eye exam can rule those out.
