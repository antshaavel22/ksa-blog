## The Road to Clear Vision

When Baruto left Estonia at 19 to pursue a professional sumo career in Japan, his eyesight wasn't a priority. Everyone in his family had good vision. Why would he be different?

Then things changed.

As a professional sumo wrestler, sharp vision was essential. Winning matches meant seeing the smallest movements of your opponent. But when Baruto greeted his competitors at the starting line, he noticed something troubling: their forms were increasingly blurry.

"At that moment, I realized my vision wasn't as sharp as I thought," he says. "I lost more than a few matches because of it."

Baruto identifies several culprits: intense physical training, stress, and the demands of professional sport. But there was something else too.

"Living in Tokyo, I didn't need to see far. Everything is close—people, buildings, constant activity right in front of you," Baruto explains. "My world had become nearsighted. When I couldn't see clearly down the street, I knew: I needed glasses."

## The Wrong Turn

"Like a typical Estonian, I didn't visit an optometrist or get my eyes properly tested," Baruto admits with a laugh. "I just bought myself some stylish designer glasses and called it a day. It worked—sort of."

But this was a mistake.

Year after year, his vision deteriorated. Stylish frames couldn't stop the decline. Eventually he did see a doctor and got a proper prescription. The new glasses helped a little. But his vision kept getting worse.

"Poor eyesight significantly affected my athletic career—and my quality of life in general," Baruto says. "I developed a habit of squinting constantly. I was always asking my assistant which wrestlers were on the mat. These were the early signs that something had to change."

For years, he managed with glasses and contacts. But the limitations wore on him. He wanted his life back. He wanted to see without aids, without compromise.
