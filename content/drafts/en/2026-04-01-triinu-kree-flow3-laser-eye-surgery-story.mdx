## Life Without Glasses—Finally

My name is Triinu Kree. I'm 19, from Viljandi originally, but Tallinn is home now. I work as an actor in local and international film and television. I coach at drama studios, write articles, perform as a poet, and work as a dramaturg and sales consultant. I'm studying dental assisting too—both my grandmothers were in dentistry. I love crimson red, feminism, pumpkin soup, and finding wonder in simple things.

But there's something else I want to share: I'm finally free from glasses. And I want to tell you why that matters so much.

## Eight Years With Glasses

I got my first pair at eight years old. I couldn't read the blackboard at school, so I'd whisper to my best friend to copy what was written. The local eye doctor was blunt: "You're practically blind." My prescription was just one diopter of nearsightedness, but it felt like a bigger deal than it was.

I hated wearing them at school. One English class, my teacher gestured at me to put them on. I remember that moment clearly—the reluctance, the self-consciousness. Over the years, I had five different pairs.

Sports were a nightmare. Skipping rope meant constant sliding. Gymnastics meant fogged lenses and broken frames. As a teenager, glasses didn't boost my confidence either. I was teased throughout school—though not always because of the glasses.

## Contact Lenses Didn't Work

In 2022, my dad and I drove to Tartu to get contact lenses fitted. I was about to take a bigger role in an international series about a shipwreck set in Estonia. I was tired of always looking different in period films—always the one with glasses on set. Everyone else dressed in black, and you could spot me by the glasses alone. I'd learned to read people by small details like that. And without glasses, when someone relaxes, their eyes squint automatically. That doesn't look good on camera.

But the contact lens experience was rough. The doctor explained that my eyes are unusually sensitive—stronger reflexes than normal. Even with every trick in the book, inserting the lenses took forever. My eyes turned red and painful. I became anxious. After filming Estonia, where I met my wonderful partner, I never tried again.

Less invasive. And the recovery is only about a week.

I booked a consultation with Dr. Ants Haavel's team. What impressed me immediately was this fact: the entire clinical team at KSA chose Flow3 for their own eyes. That tells you everything you need to know about trust. These are eye specialists—people who understand the risks and benefits better than anyone—and they chose this procedure for themselves.

My prescription was suitable for [Flow3](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/), and I decided to go ahead.

## The Procedure Itself

The day of my procedure was straightforward. No flap meant I didn't have the anxiety of worrying about my cornea being cut and repositioned. The laser does its work on the surface, which felt safer to me, especially knowing I wanted to return to sports and acting without restriction.

Recovery was faster than I expected. Within a week, my vision was clear. Within two, I was back to normal life completely.

## Life After Flow3

I'm not going to be dramatic and say everything changed overnight. But honestly? Everything did.

I can see the blackboard now. More importantly, I can act without a second thought about how my face looks on camera. No glasses catching light. No worried squinting. When I skip rope or do anything physical, there's no sliding, no fogging, no breaking.

I'm more confident. Not because glasses made me less worthy—they didn't—but because I'm no longer thinking about them. That mental freedom matters.

And the practical side: I can wake up and see. I can go to the beach without worrying about salt water damaging lenses or frames. I can be in the rain without that constant adjustment. I can exercise hard without fear.
