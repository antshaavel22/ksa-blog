Aspirin is one of the most common daily medications in the world, and for many patients it is genuinely lifesaving. It is also one of the few painkillers with a documented link to an eye condition: age-related macular degeneration.

## What the research found

A 2012 study showed a clear connection between how often you take aspirin and your risk of age-related macular degeneration (AMD), the condition that affects the macula (the central retina, where you see most sharply). Frequent aspirin users had a higher risk of both early AMD and the more serious wet late AMD.

The risk was dose-dependent: more aspirin over time, more risk. This is one finding among many, and it does not change the fact that aspirin protects the heart in patients who genuinely need it. It does change the calculation slightly for patients taking it without a clear cardiac reason.

## Aspirin's other effect on the eye surface

Aside from the macula, aspirin has a smaller, more local quirk. It can be secreted into your tears, where it sometimes forms tiny crystals in the tear film. This contributes to mild irritation or dryness, though aspirin is much less drying than ibuprofen and similar painkillers.

## What to do with this information

Do not stop your aspirin on your own. If a doctor put you on it for cardiac reasons, the cardiac protection almost certainly outweighs the macular risk for you, and stopping abruptly can be dangerous.

What is worth doing is talking to your GP and your eye specialist about it together. If you take aspirin daily out of habit rather than for a specific medical reason, that is a conversation worth having. If you do need it long-term, regular eye exams to keep an eye on your macula are the sensible response.
