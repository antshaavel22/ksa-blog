Most people think of mushrooms as a side dish. They are also one of the more interesting foods we eat. Almost every old culture used them as medicine. The Egyptians called them the plant of immortality. Modern research is quietly catching up to what those traditions noticed.

## Why mushrooms punch above their weight

Humans are genetically closer to fungi than to plants. Some of the molecules in mushrooms have been part of our diet for so long that our bodies seem to expect them. That may be part of why they do so much in such a small package.

About half of edible mushrooms are classified as functional foods, meaning they offer nutrition plus measurable health effects. They are low in calories and high in fibre, protein, B vitamins, selenium, potassium, copper, and, when grown in sunlight, vitamin D. Pale white mushrooms carry as many antioxidants as more colourful fruit and vegetables.

## What this has to do with your eyes

Your eyes do not exist in isolation from the rest of you. The retina (the back of the eye that receives the image) is metabolically very active and depends on a steady supply of antioxidants and good circulation. The macula (the central retina, where you see most sharply) ages alongside the rest of your body. Anything that supports your overall vascular and immune health tends to support your vision too.

A 2011 University of Florida study showed that daily shiitake consumption improved measurable immune function in healthy adults. The active compounds, mostly beta-glucans and polysaccharides, prime the immune cells that defend the eye surface against infection.

## A simple takeaway

Diet on its own will not fix a refractive error or reverse macular damage. It will keep the systems behind your vision working a little better for longer.
