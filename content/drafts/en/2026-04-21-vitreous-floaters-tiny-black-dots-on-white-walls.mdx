Have you ever looked up at a bright blue sky or a blank white wall and noticed tiny, semi-transparent squiggly lines or cobwebs drifting across your vision?
 Those little drifters are known as eye floaters. Today, we’re going to dive into what they are, why they happen, and what you can do about them!
What Are Eye Floaters and What Are the Symptoms? Eye floaters are tiny dark spots, threadlike strands, squiggly lines, or cobweb shapes that seem to float in front of your eyes
. They move when your eyes move, and when you try to look right at them, they quickly dart away
. While it looks like there are bugs floating in the air, what you are actually seeing are tiny shadows cast onto your retina (the light-sensitive back part of your eye) by microscopic clumps of material inside your eye
.
What Causes Them? (Etiology & Causes) To understand the cause, we have to look inside the eye. The middle of your eye is filled with a clear, jelly-like substance called the vitreous humor
. This jelly is made mostly of water, proteins called collagen, and carbohydrates
. As we get older, this jelly naturally starts to liquefy and shrink
. When it does, the microscopic collagen fibers clump together and cast those shadows we call floaters
. While aging is the most common cause, you are also at a higher risk if you are nearsighted, have had an eye injury, experience eye inflammation (uveitis), or have bleeding in the eye
.
Are They Dangerous? Most of the time, floaters are completely harmless and are just a normal part of getting older
. However, sometimes the shrinking jelly can pull so hard on the retina that it causes a retinal tear or a retinal detachment
. This is a serious medical emergency
. You should contact an eye doctor immediately if you notice a sudden shower of new floaters, flashes of light in your vision, or a gray curtain-like shadow blocking your sight
.
Do They "Melt" and How Long Does It Take? You might wonder when these annoying spots will finally melt away. The truth is, floaters do not actually melt; they are permanent and stay in your eye
. The good news is that over time—sometimes within a few months—they usually settle down to the bottom of the eye and drift out of your direct line of sight
. Plus, your amazing brain simply learns to ignore them, so you will notice them less and less
.
What Are the Treatment Options? For most people, the safest and most common treatment is simple observation—just leaving them alone
. There are no home remedies that can make them disappear
. However, if the floaters are extremely severe and make it hard to see or go about your daily life, an ophthalmologist might suggest a surgery called a vitrectomy
. During this procedure, the doctor safely removes the vitreous gel and the floaters, replacing it with a special clear solution
. Another option sometimes used is a special YAG laser to break up the floaters, though vitrectomy is the more proven and common method
.

Summary In short, eye floaters are tiny clumps of collagen in your eye's aging jelly that cast shadows on your vision
. They are usually harmless and become much less noticeable over time as they drift out of the way
. While there are surgical options for extreme cases
, the best thing you can do is maintain regular eye exams
. Remember, if you see a sudden explosion of floaters or flashes of light, seek emergency eye care right away
!
