## One Principle That Has Never Changed in Twenty Years

When Dr. Ants Haavel founded KSA Silmakeskus in 2005, the clinic was built around a single philosophy: never do more to the eye than what is truly necessary.

This wasn't learned from textbooks or conference presentations. It came from the clinic floor — from years of seeing eyes that had been over-treated, subjected to unnecessarily aggressive surgery. Eyes handled like an engineering problem, rather than the most precious sensory organ a person possesses.

"I saw patients return with corneal ectasia," Dr. Haavel explains. "It's a condition that neither glasses nor contact lenses can fix. Someone came to be free of spectacles and left with a far more serious problem. That compromise was unacceptable to me."

## Why Choose Flapless Correction?

Entering refractive surgery, Dr. Haavel faced a choice that was far from standard at the time. LASIK advocates promised rapid recovery — good vision by the next day. The flapless surface approach meant slower healing, more patient support, and more patience.

Research shows that after LASIK, the cornea retains only about 2–28% of its original structural strength. The risk of ectasia with LASIK is roughly 4.5 times higher than with flapless surface methods. [The Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) creates no permanent incision in the cornea — meaning its biomechanics remain naturally intact.

## The Number That Defines Our Work: Zero

55,000 eyes. Over 20 years. Zero cases of ectasia. Zero cases of keratoconus caused by the procedure.

According to Dr. Ants Haavel, this is no accident: "55,000 treated eyes without a single complication of this kind ranks among the most precise safety records in refractive surgery across the Baltic region. This came from combining three things: rigorous pre-operative assessment, careful patient selection, and the flapless method."

Pre-operative evaluation at KSA Silmakeskus takes 60 minutes and involves thousands of measurements — corneal topography, thickness mapping, and biomechanical analysis. Roughly 15% of people who arrive wanting surgery receive a 'no' — because the cornea is too thin, the topography is irregular, or expectations don't match what the procedure can deliver. This honesty matters as much as technical precision.

Patience is part of a good outcome. After flapless laser correction, healing takes time. The first week can feel slow. Vision stabilises gradually over days and weeks, not hours. This is not a weakness of the method — it's the price of safety.
