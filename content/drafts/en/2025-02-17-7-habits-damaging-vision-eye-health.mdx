## Your Vision Is Precious — But Daily Habits May Be Harming It

Vision is one of our most valuable senses, yet we make choices every day without realizing the damage they cause. Too much screen time. Poor nutrition. Skipped eye exams. These seemingly small decisions accumulate into serious, long-term vision problems.

The good news? Small habit changes can make a real difference.

## 1. Too Much Screen Time

Hours at your computer, phone, or TV aren't just tiring—they're actively straining your eyes. Prolonged near-focus work causes "digital eye strain": dryness, blurred vision, headaches, and neck tension.

The fix: Use the 20-20-20 rule. Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 metres away for 20 seconds. Your eyes will thank you.

## 2. Forgetting Your Sunglasses

UV radiation damages eyes even on cloudy days. Without proper protection, you're increasing your risk of cataracts and age-related macular degeneration—conditions that can severely limit vision later in life.

Invest in quality sunglasses that block at least 95% of UVA and UVB rays. Wear them year-round. It's one of the smartest preventive measures you can take.

## 3. Rubbing Your Eyes

It seems harmless, but constant eye rubbing can damage the cornea and lead to keratoconus—a condition where the cornea thins and becomes misshapen, significantly worsening vision.

If your eyes itch, use a cool compress or artificial tears instead. It's a simple swap with lasting benefits.

## 4. Sleeping in Contact Lenses

If your lenses aren't designed for overnight wear, sleeping in them multiplies your risk of eye infections and corneal ulcers—sometimes dramatically.

Simple rule: remove your lenses before bed, even if you're exhausted. It takes seconds and protects your cornea.

## 5. Poor Nutrition

Your diet directly affects your eye health. Deficiencies in vitamins A, C, and E—plus lutein, zinc, and omega-3 fatty acids—accelerate vision decline.

Eat more: leafy greens, carrots, citrus fruits, berries, flaxseeds, and algae oils. These aren't just healthy; they actively support long-term vision.

## 6. Skipping Eye Exams

Many serious eye diseases—glaucoma, retinal changes, early macular degeneration—develop silently, with no symptoms until damage is advanced. By the time you notice a problem, it may be too late.

Get your eyes checked at least once every two years. More often if you work at screens or have a family history of eye disease. Early detection can preserve vision.

## 7. Smoking (Active or Passive)

Smoking dramatically increases risk of cataracts, macular degeneration, and optic nerve damage. Even secondhand smoke can trigger dry eye syndrome.

Quitting benefits your eyes—and your overall health—within weeks. It's one of the most impactful changes you can make.
