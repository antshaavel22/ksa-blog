A good night's sleep is one of the foundations of decent health. Plenty of patients I see use sleeping pills or sedatives, occasionally or regularly, to actually get one. Then they wake up feeling like their eyelids are glued shut.

There is a reason, and it is fixable.

## Why sedatives dry the eyes

Your tear glands take their cues from nerve signals. When you take a sleeping pill or a sedative, the medication works by quieting the nerve signals between brain and body. That is what helps you sleep. It is also, unintentionally, what tells your tear glands to slow down.

Sedatives also relax muscle tone broadly, which means you blink less, both as you fall asleep and into the next day if you feel groggy. Blinking is what spreads the protective oil layer across your eye, so a slower blink rate means tears evaporating faster than usual.

## Why this matters before laser surgery

Refractive surgery depends on very precise pre-op measurements taken off the tear film. An unstable tear film smudges those readings. Recovery also depends on a moist, stable eye surface. Chronic dryness from sedatives makes both slower than they should be.

## What to do

**1. Tell us.** If you regularly take a sleeping pill or sedative, even an over-the-counter one, mention it before surgery. We plan around it.

**2. Do not stop suddenly.** If a doctor prescribed something for anxiety or insomnia, never stop it on your own. Talk to your prescribing doctor first.

**3. Hydrate at night.** Apply preservative-free artificial tears before bed and again as soon as you wake up. For overnight relief, a thicker lubricating ointment works well; it blurs vision briefly, which is fine when you are about to sleep anyway.

**4. Stick with preservative-free.** Drops with preservatives can cause cellular inflammation and make dry eye worse with daily use, which is the opposite of what you want.
