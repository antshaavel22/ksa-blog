If you take antihistamines for seasonal allergies, you know the routine. The trees start, the dust kicks up, and you reach for the tablets. They calm the runny nose and the sneezing. A few hours later, your eyes feel like someone has filled them with sand.

You are not imagining it. Allergy medications are one of the most common reasons for medication-related dry eye.

## What allergy tablets do to your tear film

Your eye surface is coated by a thin tear film made of water, oil and mucus. With each blink, that film is swept across the eye. When the film is healthy, you do not notice it. When it is disturbed, you definitely do, with grittiness, burning, redness, eyelids feeling glued shut in the morning, even fluctuating vision (a smudged tear film bends light unevenly).

Common antihistamines (diphenhydramine, loratadine, fexofenadine, cetirizine) block histamine, which is what causes allergy symptoms. Most of them also have an anticholinergic effect. In plain English, the drug also blocks the nerve signals that tell glands in your body to produce moisture. The body cannot tell the difference between the glands making your nose run and the lacrimal glands making your tears, so both go quiet.

If you wear contact lenses, the lens stops feeling comfortable. Some allergy drugs also slightly dilate the pupil and reduce focusing power, which is bothersome for younger patients trying to read.

## Why this matters before laser surgery

Refractive surgery depends on very precise measurements of the eye. The first surface the measurement light hits is your tear film. An unstable film smudges the readings, and a less precise reading means a less precise treatment. Recovery after surgery also depends on a moist eye surface, so a chronic dry eye starting from your allergy tablets makes everything slower than it needs to be.

## The fix

**1. Tell us what you take.** Prescription or over-the-counter, allergy tablets and nasal sprays both count.

**2. Do not stop on your own.** Talk to your prescribing doctor about timing or alternatives. Sometimes a simple switch makes the difference.

**3. Use the right drops.** Preservative-free artificial tears. Drops with benzalkonium chloride or other preservatives can make dry eye worse over time, which is the opposite of what you want.

**4. Blink and break.** When you take an antihistamine and then sit at a screen, your eyes are getting hit twice. A 20-second look at something far away every 20 minutes helps more than people expect.
