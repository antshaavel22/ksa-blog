## From Childhood Glasses to Clear Vision

I grew up in Viljandi, but these days I'm based in Tallinn, living what I can only describe as a beautifully chaotic creative life. I act in films and TV series—Estonian and international productions alike. I co-run drama studios, write articles, perform as a spoken-word poet, and work on screenplays. Right now I'm developing a play as a dramaturg, while juggling shifts at the Estonian Academy of Music and Theatre and studying dental assistance at the Tallinn Health Care College. Both my grandmothers worked in dentistry, so it feels like coming home.

I'm someone who finds joy in small things—scarlet red, feminism, pumpkin soup, pockets in skirts, and genuine gratitude for life's simple moments. But today, I want to share something that's genuinely changed how I experience all of it: how I finally said goodbye to glasses after 14 years.

## The Glasses Era

I got my first pair of glasses at eight years old. In class, I couldn't read the board, so I'd whisper to my neighbour (still my best friend, actually) to let me copy down what was written. That first visit to the local eye clinic was traumatic for a kid—the doctor announced, "You're practically blind!" Turns out I had a modest -1 dioptre. I was self-conscious about wearing them at school. One English lesson, my kind elderly teacher actually gestured at me to put them on. When I didn't, he walked over, crouched down, and said firmly: "Glasses." I reluctantly pulled them out and put them on.

Over my lifetime, I went through about six different pairs.

Sports were always a hassle. Jump rope? They'd slide down my nose. Sweating? They'd slip. Rain? They'd fog. Stretching or acrobatics? One fall and they'd break, meaning another replacement. And as a teenager, glasses definitely didn't help my confidence—I spent years being teased, though admittedly for more reasons than just eyewear.

## Why Contact Lenses Didn't Work

In summer 2022, my dad drove me to Tartu to try contact lenses. A major international TV series about the sinking of the MS Estonia was coming up, and I was tired of period dramas where you're constantly in glasses—it makes it impossible for the crew to tell who's who on set. I'd learned to recognise people by their walk and small details instead. Plus, on camera, your face looks more relaxed without glasses. You stop squinting. You feel more confident.

But the contact lens experience was a disappointment. My eye doctor explained I have unusually sensitive eyes with a strong protective reflex. Most people can adapt. I couldn't. The lenses felt foreign, my eyes rejected them, and within weeks it was clear this wasn't going to work.

So glasses it remained. For another couple of years, I accepted this as simply my reality.

## The Decision

Then I learned about [Flow3](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/)—a flapless surface laser procedure that sounded genuinely different. No cut. No flap. Just precision reshaping of the cornea surface. The recovery is about a week, not months. And importantly, it's considered safer for people with active lifestyles or contact sports.

I did my research. I read about KSA Silmakeskus, the clinic in Tallinn led by Dr. Ants Haavel, who's been doing this for over 20 years. What stuck with me wasn't just the numbers—55,000+ procedures—but a single detail: the entire clinical team chose Flow3 for their own eyes. That's not something a clinic can fake. If the surgeons and staff trust it enough for themselves, that means something.

I booked a consultation.

## The Procedure

I won't pretend I wasn't nervous. But the team at KSA was calm, thorough, and honest about what to expect. The [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) itself was surprisingly quick. No cutting, which immediately felt less invasive than I'd imagined. The laser does the work on the surface. It's precise. It's controlled.

The first week of recovery was exactly as described—some mild discomfort, but nothing dramatic. My vision improved daily. By day seven, it was stable and clear.

## Life After

It's strange to describe, but colours are genuinely brighter. Not in some magical, exaggerated way—but in the simple, profound way that happens when you're no longer looking at the world through glass and plastic.

I can wake up and see. Immediately. No reaching for glasses. No ritual of checking where they are. No fog, no slipping, no breaks. I can train without worry. I can perform without the self-consciousness I never quite shook. I can work on film sets and see faces clearly, see details, see the scene as the cinematographer intended.

Would I recommend it? Absolutely—but only after talking to a professional who can assess your specific situation. Not everyone is a candidate. Some people might be better suited to [ICB lens replacement](https://ksa.ee/en/icb-time) instead. That's why the consultation matters.

What I will say is this: if you've spent most of your life in glasses, if you've wondered what it would be like to not need them, if you're tired of the small daily frustrations—it's worth exploring seriously.

I'm grateful I did.
