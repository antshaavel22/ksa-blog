## 8–12 minutes every single day. Sounds small? Think again.

You wake up. Search the bedside table for your glasses—or was it the desk? Frame them on your nose. Wipe them clean because they're already fogged. At night, case them up. If you wear contacts: wash your hands, open the case, solution, insert, remove at night, rinse, fresh solution. Every day. Every morning and every evening. For years. For decades.

On its own, it's nothing dramatic—8 to 12 minutes a day. But multiply that by 365 days, then by 30 years, and you get a number that stops you cold: **more than 100 days of your life** vanishes into glasses and contact lens routine. That's longer than three months—24 hours a day, round the clock.

## The lost glasses hunt marathon

According to the American Optometric Association, **27% of glasses wearers lose their glasses at least once a week**. Average search time? 5 minutes and 20 seconds. Seems trivial until you do the maths:

* Once a week × 5 min 20 secs = roughly **4.6 hours per year**
* But most people lose them more often—realistically 2–3 times weekly
* That's up to **17 hours annually** just hunting for glasses—more than two working days

And that's only the searching. Add the morning cleaning ritual, wiping fogged lenses in Estonian winter, the morning and evening contact lens prep—and the time drain becomes undeniable.

## Small daily irritations you've learned to ignore

Glasses and contacts aren't just time thieves. They're a thousand tiny frustrations you've grown so used to that you barely notice them anymore:

* **Fogged lenses coming in from the cold**—walking into a shop, opening the oven, the sauna. In Estonia, where winter lasts half the year, it's a daily reality.
* **Raindrops on your lenses**—seeing the world through a blurred veil of water. The lens-wipe gesture is automatic now.
* **Can't jump in the pool**—spontaneous swimming? Forget it without your lenses. Kids calling from the water while you're hunting for your aids.
* **Contact solution at the airport**—the 100 ml rule, a separate bag, the mental load. Forget it and you arrive lens-free.
* **Dry eyes at day's end**—after 10 hours of lens wear, your eyes burn red and parched. You burn through eye drops faster than you'd like.
* **Falling asleep with lenses in**—wake up and your eyes feel like sandpaper. Familiar?
* **Training in glasses**—they slip, they slide, they fog with sweat. Every squat means adjusting them.

You've adapted. But should you have to?
