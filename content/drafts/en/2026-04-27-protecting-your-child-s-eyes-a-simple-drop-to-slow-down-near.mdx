As parents, we carefully watch every step of our children's development. But have you noticed your child squinting at the TV, or holding their books and tablets too close to their face?
Nearsightedness, also known as myopia, is increasing rapidly across the globe. In fact, experts predict that by the year 2050, half of the world's population will be myopic, affecting more than 740 million children and adolescents
. While getting a pair of glasses might seem like a simple fix, childhood myopia is actually a serious eye health concern.
Why Should We Stop Myopia from Growing? When a child becomes nearsighted, their eyeball is physically growing too long. If the eyeball stretches beyond 26 millimetres, the retina cannot function properly
. Over time, this extreme stretching significantly increases the risk of severe eye diseases in adulthood, such as glaucoma, cataracts, and retinal detachment
. Because of this, actively controlling myopia progression is now a vital healthcare strategy
.
The Magic in a Drop: Low-Concentration Atropine For years, doctors have searched for the safest, most effective way to slow down this eyeball growth. The answer lies in a simple, low-concentration eye drop called atropine.
Understanding the Difference: 0.05% vs. 0.01% Drops A major multi-year clinical study known as the LAMP Study tested different daily doses of atropine (including 0.05% and 0.01%) on children to see which worked best
. While both concentrations are very low and well-tolerated by children, the results clearly showed that the 0.05% atropine concentration is the absolute "sweet spot" for children
.
The studies revealed that 0.05% atropine is twice as effective as the 0.01% drops at stopping myopia progression and eyeball stretching over a two- to three-year period
. While the 0.01% concentration does offer some help, it is significantly less effective
. Because 0.05% offers maximum protection with minimal side effects, it is widely considered the optimal choice for long-term treatment
.
Are These Drops Safe for My Child? Absolutely. Studies confirm that these low concentrations are very well tolerated and safe
. Because atropine can cause the pupils to dilate (widen) very slightly, some children might experience mild light sensitivity
. Fortunately, photochromic glasses (lenses that automatically darken outdoors) provide perfect comfort for these children
. Most importantly, the drops do not affect your child's overall quality of life or their ability to read clearly
.
What is the "Rebound Effect"? Treating myopia is a marathon, not a sprint. If you suddenly stop using the daily eye drops, the child's nearsightedness can speed up again—a phenomenon doctors call the "rebound effect"
.
Research shows that stopping the drops when a child is still young leads to a much faster rebound in their myopia
. While slightly stronger concentrations like 0.05% can have a mathematically larger rebound than 0.01%, medical experts note that the actual difference between the two is so small it doesn't matter clinically
. Because the 0.05% drops work so much better overall, they remain the best choice
. To prevent a strong rebound, doctors recommend continuing the treatment until your child is older and their natural eye growth has slowed down
.
The Power of Outdoor Time While atropine drops are a powerful medical tool, healthy daily habits are just as important. Daily exposure to natural outdoor light has strong scientific support in protecting children from developing severe myopia
.
Encourage your children to spend time outdoors in natural daylight every day
. Additionally, make sure they take visual breaks every 30 minutes, closely manage their screen time, and avoid reading in dim light
.
