Most people reach for a painkiller without thinking about their eyes. Most of the time that is fine. There are a few specific connections worth knowing about, especially if you take painkillers regularly or you are heading into laser eye surgery.

## What everyday painkillers do to the tear film

The painkillers people use most are NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs), and the most common is ibuprofen. They work everywhere in the body, not just on the sore knee. While they are calming inflammation, they can also destabilise your tear film, the thin watery and oily layer that coats the eye.

Not everyone gets dry eye from ibuprofen, but it is a real and well-documented effect. Tears evaporate faster than they should, and the eye starts to feel gritty, burning or irritated.

## A look at prescription anti-inflammatories

Sometimes the over-the-counter painkillers are not enough, and doctors prescribe stronger anti-inflammatories. Celecoxib (Celebrex) and meloxicam (Mobic) belong to a class called COX-2 inhibitors. They have their own eye effects: blurred vision and conjunctivitis (inflammation of the clear membrane covering the white of the eye) are documented side effects.

## The aspirin story

Aspirin is in a category of its own because many patients take it daily as a blood thinner. Two specific effects are worth flagging:

**Bleeding under the conjunctiva.** Because aspirin and other blood thinners (clopidogrel, warfarin) prolong bleeding times, completely harmless burst blood vessels on the eye surface (subconjunctival haemorrhages) look much more dramatic and last longer than they would otherwise. They are not dangerous, just visually alarming.

**Macular degeneration risk.** Frequent and long-term aspirin use has been linked, in research, to an increased risk of age-related macular degeneration, the condition that affects the macula (the central retina, where you see most sharply).

## Why this matters before laser surgery

Refractive surgery depends on a stable tear film for accurate pre-op measurements and on a healthy eye surface for smooth recovery. Chronic dryness from regular ibuprofen or COX-2 use makes both harder than they need to be.

For eyelid surgery specifically, your surgeon may ask you to pause aspirin or other blood thinners temporarily to reduce bleeding. This is always coordinated with the doctor who prescribed them in the first place.

## A practical plan

**1. Tell us what you take.** Even occasional over-the-counter ibuprofen.

**2. Do not stop prescribed medication on your own.** If you are on a blood thinner or strong anti-inflammatory for a real reason, the eye specialist talks to your prescribing doctor before anything changes.

**3. Use preservative-free artificial tears.** These help with the dryness without adding new irritation.

**4. Watch for changes.** New blurred vision or persistent redness after starting a painkiller is worth a phone call to your doctor.
