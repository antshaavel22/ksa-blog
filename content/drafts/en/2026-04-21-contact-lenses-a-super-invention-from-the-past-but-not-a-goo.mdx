Contact lenses are one of the more underrated inventions of modern medicine. We went from awkward early prototypes to breathable silicone hydrogels and daily disposables that give you a fresh, sterile lens every morning. They give you peripheral vision for sport, freedom from glasses, and a kind of confidence that is hard to put a price on.

What they are not good for is water.

## The Acanthamoeba problem

Pool, lake, sea, and even a shower at home: any time you put water on a contact lens, you put your eye at risk. Pool chlorine strips your eye's natural tear film, which leaves the surface red and vulnerable. Salt water dehydrates the lens. Lake water carries everything from algae to runoff.

The most dangerous resident in all of these is a parasite called Acanthamoeba. It exists in two forms: an active feeding form and a tough cyst form that survives chlorine. When it gets trapped behind a contact lens, it can invade the cornea (the eye's clear front layer) and cause Acanthamoeba keratitis.

The early symptoms are easy to mistake for ordinary irritation: discomfort, redness, watering, light sensitivity, the feeling of something stuck in the eye. As the parasite gets deeper into the cornea it reaches the nerves, and the pain becomes severe. Treatment typically takes months, sometimes more than a year, and can still end in permanent vision loss or a corneal transplant. Around 90% of cases happen to contact lens wearers.

## Tap water is not safe either

Patients sometimes ask whether they can rinse the lens case in tap water if they are not actually swimming. The answer is no. Tap water is not sterile and can carry the same Acanthamoeba. Always clean your case with multi-purpose solution, shake it out, and let it air-dry. Replace it every three months.

## What to do instead

You do not have to choose between clear vision and being in the water. Three options work well:

**1. Prescription swimming goggles.** Inexpensive, sealed, and effective. They keep contaminated water away from your eyes entirely.

**2. Ortho-K lenses.** Rigid lenses you wear only at night. While you sleep they gently reshape the cornea, and you wake up seeing clearly without anything in your eye during the day, including in the pool.

**3. A modern laser procedure.** [Flow3](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) removes the need for any correction. It is a one-time procedure with about a 1-week recovery before you can swim again.

## The takeaway

Enjoy your contacts. They are an excellent piece of technology and they make daily life much easier. Just keep them and their case away from water of any kind. If swimming with clear vision matters to you, prescription goggles, Ortho-K, or a [laser procedure](https://ksa.ee) all solve the problem without putting your cornea at risk.
