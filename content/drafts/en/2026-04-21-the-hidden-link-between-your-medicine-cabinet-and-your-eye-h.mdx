Eyes feeling dry, gritty or tired by the end of the day? Most people blame screens and sleep. The real culprit is often sitting on the bathroom shelf. From allergy tablets to blood pressure pills, several common medications quietly change the way your eyes produce and hold tears.

Knowing which ones matter is especially worth it before any laser eye procedure.

## Why this happens

Your eye surface depends on a tear film made of water, oil and mucus. Many medications disrupt this film by reducing how many tears you make, or by changing what they are made of. The eye does not get to choose which signals get blocked. Whatever the drug does to other glands in the body, it tends to do to the lacrimal and meibomian glands too.

The most common offenders:

**Antihistamines.** They stop the sneezing. They also stop the eye from producing enough moisture.

**Antidepressants and anxiety medications.** Older tricyclics in particular interfere with the nerve signals that tell your tear glands to fire. Newer SSRIs do this less, but still cause dry eye in some patients.

**Blood pressure medications.** Beta-blockers and diuretics reduce tear production and shift the salt-water balance of your tears.

**Hormonal medications.** Birth control pills and hormone replacement therapy alter tear composition and stability. Estrogen-only therapy is harder on the eye than combined therapy.

## A note on isotretinoin

Strong acne medications such as isotretinoin (Accutane, Absorica) deserve their own paragraph. They work by shutting down oil production in the skin. The same drugs also shut down the meibomian glands in the eyelids, which produce the oily layer of your tears. Without that layer, tears evaporate almost instantly. Patients on isotretinoin frequently end up with significant dry eye and inflammation of the eye surface.

## Why this matters for laser surgery

Refractive surgery depends on very precise measurements of the eye. The first surface the measurement light hits is your tear film. If the tear film is unstable, the measurements are less accurate, and the treatment is less precise. Recovery also depends on a healthy, moist surface.

For isotretinoin specifically, the standard rule at quality clinics is to pause the medication at least two weeks before surgery and avoid it for four weeks afterwards. Your dermatologist needs to be in that conversation.

## A simple plan

**1. Make a complete list.** Prescription drugs, over-the-counter remedies, vitamins, supplements. All of it.

**2. Tell your eye specialist.** Even the things that seem unrelated to eyes. We have seen most of them.

**3. Do not stop on your own.** Adjusting prescription medication is a job for your prescribing doctor, not a guess.

**4. Prepare the surface.** Preservative-free artificial tears, used regularly, get the eye in shape before any procedure.
