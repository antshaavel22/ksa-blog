When patients think about hormones, they think about mood, skin and energy. Hormones also have a quiet, consistent effect on the eyes, and most people are never told about it.

## Why hormones reach the tear film

Your eye surface is coated by a tear film made of water, oil and mucus. Your natural hormonal balance helps keep that film stable. When you change the hormone mix with a medication, the tear film often shifts with it.

The most common patterns:

**Birth control pills and HRT.** Oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapy used during menopause are widely associated with dry eye. They alter tear composition and reduce tear stability, leading to dryness, irritation and a gritty feeling.

**Estrogen versus combined therapy.** Patients on estrogen-only hormone therapy report dry eye more often than patients on combined estrogen and progesterone. The mechanism is not fully understood, but the pattern is consistent.

**Tamoxifen (Nolvadex).** Used for breast and ovarian cancer, this is an estrogen receptor modulator with specific eye effects. At higher doses over longer periods it can cause opaque spots on the cornea (the eye's clear front layer), crystalline deposits in the retina (the back of the eye that receives the image), or swelling of the macula (the central retina, where you see most sharply). Patients on tamoxifen need regular eye exams.

## Why this matters before laser surgery

Refractive surgery depends on very precise measurements taken off the tear film. Hormonally driven dry eye smudges the readings, which makes the treatment less precise. Recovery after surgery also depends on a moist, stable eye surface, so chronic dryness slows healing down.

## A simple plan

**1. Tell us everything.** Birth control, HRT, supplements: write the full list before your appointment.

**2. Do not stop on your own.** Adjusting hormone medication is a job for your prescribing doctor.

**3. Use preservative-free drops.** Standard drops with preservatives can make dry eye worse with daily use. Preservative-free is the right default.

**4. If you take tamoxifen, schedule yearly eye exams.** Catching small retinal changes early matters.
