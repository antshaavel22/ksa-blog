Eye pain that actually originates from your sinuses is a classic example of "referred pain." This occurs because the nerve branches from both your eyes and your sinuses converge along the trigeminal nerve pathway
. Because these signals travel the same route, the brain sometimes misinterprets sinus pressure as pain coming from the eye itself
.
You can identify if your eye pain is likely referred from your sinuses by looking for a few specific indicators:
Changes with movement: The most distinct sign of sinus-related eye pain is that the headache or facial soreness worsens or changes when you adjust your head position
.
Location of the pain: The discomfort is typically felt deep behind the eyes, rather than on the surface of the eye
.
Appearance of the eye: Unlike many eye-specific issues (like infections or abrasions), the eye area itself will usually look completely normal, though some individuals might experience a bit of swelling around the outside of the eye
.
While sinus pressure is a common cause of referred pain, you should consult a doctor if the pain is severe, does not improve, progressively worsens, or is accompanied by other symptoms like vision changes, noticeable redness, or nausea
