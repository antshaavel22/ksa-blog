Summer trips, weekend spas and the local swimming pool all share one thing: the water. If you wear contacts, that water is the problem. It seems convenient to leave the lenses in so you can see the edge of the pool clearly. Eye care professionals and the FDA say no for a reason.

## The sponge effect

Soft contact lenses are made from porous plastic hydrogels or silicone hydrogels. They are designed to absorb moisture and stay flexible on the eye. That is great in normal conditions. In a pool or a lake, it turns into a hazard. The lens absorbs whatever is in the water and traps it directly against your cornea (the eye's clear front layer).

Even water that looks crystal clear is not sterile. When microbes are pinned against the eye by a lens, they have a warm wet environment to multiply, and your tear film, which would normally wash them away, is blocked.

## Acanthamoeba keratitis: the worst case

The most feared infection from this is Acanthamoeba keratitis. Acanthamoeba is a microscopic parasite that lives in tap water, lakes, rivers and pools. When it gets onto the cornea, it causes pain that is often out of all proportion to the visible signs in the eye, extreme light sensitivity, and over time, permanent scarring. Treatment can take a year or more, and some patients still need a corneal transplant.

## Pools, lakes and oceans: pick your hazard

A common myth is that chlorinated pools are safe because the chlorine kills bacteria. Chlorine reduces bacteria but does not eliminate everything, and Acanthamoeba is one of the parasites that survives it. The chlorine itself is also an irritant, and a contact lens will hold it against your eye for hours.

Sea water is heavily salted and dehydrates the lens and the eye quickly. It also contains plenty of bacteria and viruses that cause eye infections. Lakes and rivers are the highest-risk category of all, because slow-moving freshwater can carry runoff, sewage, algae and a high concentration of parasites.

## What to do if water gets in

Do not panic, but act quickly:

**1. Take the lenses out immediately.** This is the single most important step.

**2. Throw them away.** If they are dailies, bin them. If they are reusables, the safest option is also to throw them away; at minimum, clean and disinfect them in solution overnight. Never rinse them in tap water.

**3. Rinse the eye.** Use sterile, preservative-free artificial tears.

**4. Watch for symptoms.** Severe pain, persistent redness, abnormal discharge or extreme light sensitivity all need urgent attention from an eye doctor.

## After laser surgery

After any procedure that involves an incision in the cornea (LASIK, SMILE), the eyes are more vulnerable to water-borne infection during recovery. With Flow3, surface healing takes about a week before swimming is safe. Whichever procedure you have, follow the timeline your surgeon gives you.

## The long-term answer

Prescription swim goggles are an excellent short-term fix. If you swim often and want to be free of the problem entirely, a [laser eye procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) removes the need for lenses altogether. Once you are healed and cleared by your surgeon, you can swim without thinking about your eyes at all. That is, in the end, the point of doing it.
