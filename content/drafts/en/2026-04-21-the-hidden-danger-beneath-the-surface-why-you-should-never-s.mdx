Summer holidays, weekend spa getaways, and regular trips to the local swimming pool all share one glorious thing: the water. But if you are one of the millions of people who rely on contact lenses for clear vision, the water hides a significant, unseen risk. While it might seem incredibly convenient to leave your lenses in so you can clearly see the edge of the pool or the approaching waves, eye care professionals and the FDA strictly advise against exposing your contact lenses to any type of water
.
The "Sponge" Effect To understand why swimming with lenses is so dangerous, we need to look at how soft contact lenses are made. Soft lenses, typically made from plastic hydrogels or silicone hydrogels, are inherently porous
. They are designed to absorb moisture to stay flexible and comfortable
. However, when you enter a pool, lake, or ocean, this helpful feature turns into a severe hazard. The contact lens acts like a microscopic sponge, absorbing whatever is in the water and trapping it directly against the sensitive surface of your eye
.
Even when water looks crystal clear, it is never completely sterile
. When trapped by a contact lens, waterborne microbes are given a warm, moist environment to multiply rapidly
. This bypasses your eye’s natural defence mechanism—the tear film, which would normally wash harmful pathogens away
.
The Greatest Threat: Acanthamoeba Keratitis One of the most severe risks of swimming with contacts is a microscopic parasite called Acanthamoeba, which is commonly found in tap water, freshwater lakes, and rivers
. When this organism attaches to a contact lens, it can invade the cornea, leading to a condition known as Acanthamoeba keratitis
. This infection is devastating. It causes excruciating pain that is often out of proportion to early physical signs, extreme sensitivity to light, and can ultimately lead to permanent vision loss or the need for a corneal transplant
.
Pools, Lakes, and Oceans: Pick Your Poison Many people assume that chlorinated swimming pools are safe because the chemicals kill bacteria. Sadly, this is a myth
.
Swimming Pools: Chlorine reduces bacteria but does not kill all pathogens, particularly hardy organisms like Acanthamoeba
. Furthermore, pool chemicals themselves are severe eye irritants. The lens can absorb the chlorine, holding it against your eye and causing severe chemical irritation, redness, and dry eye
.
The Ocean: Seawater is heavily concentrated with salt, which will rapidly dehydrate your lenses and eyes, leading to intense stinging and discomfort
. Furthermore, scientists have found massive amounts of bacteria and viruses in ocean water that are known to cause eye infections
.
Lakes and Rivers: Freshwater poses the highest risk of all. Stagnant or slow-moving natural water is often contaminated with agricultural run-off, sewage spills, algae, and a dangerously high concentration of parasites
.
What To Do If Water Gets in Your Eyes If you accidentally splash water into your eyes or forget to remove your lenses before jumping in, don't panic, but act swiftly:
Remove the lenses immediately. This is the most crucial step to prevent pathogens from adhering to your eyes
.
Throw them away. If you wear daily disposables, bin them immediately
. If you wear reusables, it is safest to throw them away, but at the very least, they must be rigorously cleaned and disinfected in a proper solution overnight
. Never rinse your lenses in tap water
.
Flush your eyes. Use sterile, preservative-free artificial tears to gently rinse out any lingering chemicals or debris
.
Monitor for symptoms. If you develop severe pain, redness that won't go away, abnormal discharge, or light sensitivity, seek emergency eye care immediately
.
After Eye Surgery Following vision-correcting laser procedures that involve an incision in the cornea (such as LASIK and SMILE), your eyes are considerably more sensitive and susceptible to waterborne infections
. During the recovery period from these operations, you should not open your eyes underwater, as water entering the eye can prolong the healing process and significantly increase the risk of infection
.
The Ultimate Solutions for Swimmers You shouldn't have to choose between your eye health and enjoying the water. For temporary relief, prescription swimming goggles are a fantastic investment, offering a watertight seal and clear vision without the risk of water-related infections
.
However, if you want total freedom, laser eye surgery is the ultimate long-term solution
. Refractive surgery reshapes your cornea, completely eliminating the need for glasses or contact lenses
. Once you are fully recovered from the procedure and cleared by your doctor, you can dive, swim, and splash with absolute freedom and zero risk of contact-lens-related infections
.
