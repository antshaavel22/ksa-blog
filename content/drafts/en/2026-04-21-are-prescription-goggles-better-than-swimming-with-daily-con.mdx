If you wear glasses and want to see clearly while you swim, you have two options. You can wear prescription goggles, or you can leave your daily contacts in. The first is much safer than the second, and the gap is bigger than most people realise.

## Why goggles are the safer choice

Prescription goggles are made to your prescription and seal against the skin, so chlorine, salt and bacteria stay out of your eyes. Because no water reaches the eye, the main risk of swimming with corrected vision (water-borne infection) is removed entirely. They also last for years and cost roughly 20 to 40 euros, so they pay for themselves quickly compared with a constant supply of dailies. Some pairs add UV protection or anti-fog coatings.

## Why daily contacts in the pool are a real risk

Some people are told that wearing dailies under tight goggles is a reasonable backup. That reduces the risk, but it does not remove it. Standard swim goggles leak. Even a small amount of non-sterile water gets in, and a soft contact lens behaves like a sponge. It absorbs the water and presses microbes directly against your cornea (the eye's clear front layer).

The worst-case scenario is Acanthamoeba keratitis, an infection caused by a parasite that lives in tap water, lakes and pools. It is very hard to treat, often takes more than a year of intensive therapy, and can leave permanent vision loss. About 90% of cases occur in contact lens wearers, and water exposure is the main trigger.

## If you do swim with lenses

Take them out and throw them away the moment you are out of the water. Do not save them, do not rinse them in tap water, and do not put them back in. The longer they sit on your eye after exposure, the more chance any microbes have to multiply.

The cleanest answer is to leave the lenses in their case and wear prescription goggles.
