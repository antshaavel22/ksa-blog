## Meet Taimi

Taimi is the kind of person who moves through life at full speed. She organises events. She dances—Zouk, Bachata, the kind of dancing that demands everything from your body. She practises acro-yoga. She travels the world. Her life wasn't built for sitting still.

For 17 years, it was also built around vision correction. Glasses since year five. Then contact lenses, which at least stayed on her face during intense movement. At least most of the time.

But glasses and contacts have a way of complicating a life like Taimi's. In the sauna with friends? Can't wear them. In the pool? A single misstep on the waterslide and—one contact lens gone. She remembers that moment at the water park with dry humour: standing there, half-blind, forced to choose between removing the other lens or continuing on as a blur. "I laughed about it," she says. "But it also showed me how exhausting this really was."

She'd been dreaming about laser eye surgery for years.

## How the Dream Took Shape

Taimi decided she'd have the procedure done at home in Tallinn. No long recovery journeys. No traveling across borders with healing eyes. And when she started researching clinics, the people she trusted—friends, family—kept mentioning the same name: KSA Silmakeskus.

It wasn't just the expertise that drew her in. It was the atmosphere. People told her that the staff actually cares about your wellbeing *after* the procedure too. That mattered to her.

But the procedure has a cost, and Taimi's dream needed help to become real.

Three people changed the trajectory of her life. A friend in India sold her possessions to contribute. Her best friend—a writer who shared her values and vision—stepped in. And her partner, seeing how much this meant to her, covered months of rent so she could save what she needed.

That kind of love doesn't fade. Taimi still carries the gratitude of it.

## The First Week: Recovery Rediscovered

The days after the [Flow3 procedure](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) were quiet and gentle. Her eyes were tired—that was natural. But in that tiredness came something unexpected: the chance to really *feel* other things.

Blueberries burst on her tongue. Raspberries melted like sugar. Friends came over. They sang power songs together, built puzzles, ate pancakes.

One week later, she was back at work. Back at dance training. Three weeks later, behind the wheel again.

Taimi's story isn't unusual at KSA. It's the story of someone who realised that vision correction shouldn't demand compromise. That your eyes shouldn't dictate the boundaries of your life.

Today, she dances without thinking about whether her contacts will stay put. She travels without packing solution bottles. She swims without counting the seconds before she needs to adjust or remove her lenses.
