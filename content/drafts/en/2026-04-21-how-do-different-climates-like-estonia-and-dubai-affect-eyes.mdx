Step out of a heated apartment into a Tallinn morning in January, and your eyes water. Step out of an air-conditioned skyscraper into a Dubai afternoon in July, and they sting. The two climates are nothing alike, and yet your eyes complain in almost the same way.

The reason is simple. Of all your organs, only your eyes are in constant direct contact with the air. Whatever the air is doing, your eyes feel it.

## The thin film that protects your eye

Your eye surface is coated with a tear film roughly 7 to 9 nanometres thick. It is your first defence against infection, the layer that delivers oxygen and nutrients to the cornea (the eye's clear front layer), and the brush that sweeps debris away.

The tear film has three layers. The outer one is oily, made by the meibomian glands at the edge of the eyelids; it stops the watery layer from evaporating. The middle layer is water, made by the lacrimal glands, and washes away particles. The inner layer is mucus, made by goblet cells in the conjunctiva, and helps the watery layer stick to the eye evenly.

When any one of these three layers fails, the tear film breaks up and you get dry eye disease.

## The Estonian winter

Cold air holds very little water. When you breathe Estonian January air, the dry air outside pulls moisture out of your tear film much faster than usual. Step inside, and central heating finishes the job. A Japanese study tracking dry-eye surgery rates found a clear seasonal spike in winter.

The result is a chain reaction. The oily top layer evaporates too quickly. The lacrimal glands panic and produce a flood of watery emergency tears, which without the proper oil and mucus balance just run down your cheeks. So you end up with two complaints at once: eyes that feel dry and eyes that water constantly. Both are the same problem.

## The Dubai summer

A desert summer is the same problem from the other end. Outdoor heat and strong UV light speed up tear evaporation directly. Indoors, air conditioning pulls moisture out of the air just like central heating does in Tallinn. AC vents blowing on your face are particularly hard on the eyes; they sweep the tear film off in seconds.

The desert adds a physical irritant on top of all that: blowing sand and dust that abrade the eye surface and leave you with redness and stringy mucus.

## Air pollution: the modern variable

Both climates share one more factor that quietly makes everything worse: traffic and industrial emissions. Studies show a clear correlation between dry-eye symptoms and nitrogen oxide levels, and between dry eye and fine particulates. The pollutants set off allergic and inflammatory reactions on the conjunctiva, on top of whatever the climate is already doing.

## Your biology and your habits

Climate sets the stage, but your habits decide how bad it gets. Looking at a screen drops your blink rate sharply, which leaves your eyes exposed to dry air for longer. Aircraft cabins run below 20% humidity, which is harsher than either Tallinn or Dubai. Tear production drops naturally after about 50, and women are more affected because of hormonal changes. Some routine medications, including antihistamines, beta-blockers and antidepressants, also reduce tear production.

## What actually helps

Aim for indoor humidity between 40% and 60%. A bedroom humidifier is one of the best small investments you can make. Wraparound sunglasses outdoors give you a physical barrier against wind, sun and pollution. When working at a screen, take breaks and lower the screen below eye level so your eyelids open less. A diet with enough vitamin A and omega-3 fats supports the oily layer of your tears from the inside.

If lifestyle changes are not enough, an eye doctor can identify which layer of the tear film is failing using a Schirmer's test (paper strips to measure tear volume) or a tear breakup time test (a dye that shows how quickly tears evaporate). Mild symptoms usually respond to preservative-free artificial tears. For overnight relief, a thicker lubricating ointment helps. Persistent cases may need a prescription such as cyclosporine or lifitegrast. In severe cases, tiny silicone plugs in the tear ducts (punctal occlusion) keep what tears you have on the eye for longer.

## The takeaway

Whether the air outside is freezing or scorching, your comfort depends on a microscopic layer of oil, water and mucus staying intact. Once you understand that, the fixes are much less mysterious.
