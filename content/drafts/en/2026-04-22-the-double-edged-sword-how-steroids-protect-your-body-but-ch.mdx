Corticosteroids (prednisone, hydrocortisone, betamethasone and the rest) are some of the most useful drugs in modern medicine. They keep asthma, allergies, autoimmune disease and many serious inflammations under control. For some patients they are genuinely the difference between living well and not living at all.

They also do real things inside the eye. Patients are rarely told this, and it matters whether the steroid comes as a pill, an inhaler, a nasal spray, a skin cream or an IV.

## Three things steroids do to the eye

**1. Steroid-induced high eye pressure (and glaucoma).** Inside your eye, fluid is constantly being made and drained. Steroids interfere with the drainage, so pressure builds up. Patients whose pressure spikes from this are called steroid responders. The spike usually appears after about two weeks of continuous use. High eye pressure has no symptoms, so you will not feel it. Left unchecked, it damages the optic nerve, which is glaucoma.

**2. Posterior subcapsular cataracts.** Most cataracts (clouding of the eye's natural lens) develop slowly over decades. Long-term steroid use causes a specific kind that develops on the back of the lens and forms surprisingly fast, sometimes within weeks. It causes blurred vision, glare and difficulty in bright light.

**3. Central serous retinopathy.** In some patients, steroids cause fluid to leak under the retina. It creates a small blister that blurs central vision. It is usually reversible once the steroid is stopped.

## Why this matters before laser surgery

If long-term steroid use has already started a cataract, laser refractive surgery is not the right answer. Cataract surgery is. We will see this on the exam and tell you straight.

If you are a steroid responder, your surgeon needs to know before any procedure. After many eye operations, mild steroid drops are part of the standard healing protocol. If your eye pressure spikes in response to those drops, we monitor you closely and adjust the regimen so your optic nerve stays safe.

## A practical plan

**1. Never stop a steroid suddenly.** Abrupt withdrawal can be dangerous. Always taper under your doctor's instructions.

**2. Get regular eye exams.** If you are on long-term steroids in any form (yes, including the asthma inhaler and the nasal spray), have your eye pressure checked regularly. Silent pressure spikes are exactly the kind of thing routine exams catch.

**3. Tell us about every form.** Pills, inhalers, sprays, creams, drops, joint injections. All of it counts.

**4. Do not ignore visual changes.** Glare, halos around lights, or a sudden drop in vision while on steroids is not a wait-and-see situation. Call your eye doctor.
