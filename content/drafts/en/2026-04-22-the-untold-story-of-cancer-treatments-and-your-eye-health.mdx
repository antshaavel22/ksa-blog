Cancer treatment asks a lot of you. Energy, time, patience. The eyes are usually the last thing on the list. They are also often the first to give a quiet signal that something has shifted.

I see plenty of patients during and after chemotherapy who arrive with dry eye, blurred vision or unexpected changes in their sight. The connection between drug and eye is usually direct, not mysterious, and most of these problems are manageable if you catch them early.

## Why cancer drugs reach the eyes

Cancer treatments are designed to attack rapidly dividing cells or to target specific proteins. The cells on the eye surface and in the tear glands also turn over fast, which puts them in the line of fire.

The result is most often dry eye: a feeling of grit, burning or persistent tiredness in the eyes. Some drugs leave longer-lasting marks on the retina (the back of the eye that receives the image) or on the cornea (the eye's clear front layer).

## The drugs to keep an eye on

**Classical chemotherapy (taxanes).** Docetaxel and paclitaxel act directly on the tear glands. Tear production drops, the eye surface dries out. Most of this recovers within a few months after treatment ends.

**Targeted therapies and immunotherapy.** Newer drugs (immune checkpoint inhibitors, EGFR and FGFR inhibitors) can also damage the eye surface. Belantamab mafodotin (used in multiple myeloma) deserves a separate mention: regular eye monitoring is part of the standard protocol throughout treatment.

**Tamoxifen (Nolvadex).** Used in breast and ovarian cancer. At higher doses over long periods, it can cause small opaque spots on the cornea, crystalline deposits in the retina, and sometimes swelling of the macula (the central retina, where you see most sharply). Patients on tamoxifen need at least yearly eye exams.

**Interferon-α.** Used in melanoma, leukaemia and certain lymphomas. It can cause interferon retinopathy, a pattern of small retinal haemorrhages and cotton-wool spots. Often it has no symptoms early on. If you are starting interferon, schedule an eye exam at the start of treatment and again a few months in.

## When to come in

Contact an eye doctor if:

- your eyes are constantly dry or burning and artificial tears no longer help
- vision becomes blurred or distorted (straight lines look bent)
- new black spots, "flies" or flashes appear in your field of view
- one eye is starting to see clearly differently from the other

These are usually not life-threatening, but they need a same-week look.

## What about laser surgery later?

Many of my patients come back to us after recovery from cancer treatment, looking to finally be free of glasses. That is absolutely possible, just not immediately.

Before [laser surgery](https://ksa.ee/en/laser-eye-surgery/) the eye surface needs to be calm and healthy. Dry eye smudges the pre-op measurements like a dirty window. Healing after surgery also depends on a stable tear film. We usually wait until cancer treatment is finished and the eye has been settled for several months. The exact timing is decided at the consultation.

## A simple plan

**1. Tell us about every drug.** Including the ones you stopped years ago. Some effects show up later.

**2. Use preservative-free artificial tears.** They are gentle for daily use. Drops with preservatives can make irritation worse.

**3. Stay on schedule with eye exams.** If you are on tamoxifen, interferon, or strong chemotherapy, dilated eye exams at least once a year are not optional. The first year of treatment often needs more frequent checks.

**4. Do not stop cancer treatment on your own because of your eyes.** That decision belongs to your oncologist. Our job is to keep your eyes comfortable through the treatment.

Cancer treatment is hard enough. Worrying about your eyes does not need to be added to the list. If something is bothering you, [come in](https://ksa.ee) and we will look at what is going on and what can be done.
